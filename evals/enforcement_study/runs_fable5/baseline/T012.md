The reported efficacy is only weakly supported, and low confidence is warranted. The main problem is a citation mismatch.

The cited source does not support the claim. Material 0 attributes a "52% reduction across 19 states in a large meta-analysis" to Paul et al. (2018), but reference [1] for that citation is "Baseline sensitivity and genetic structure of Fusarium graminearum populations" (Phytopathology 108:873-882). That is a study of fungicide sensitivity and population genetics of the pathogen, not a multi-state meta-analysis of field efficacy. A paper on baseline sensitivity and genetic structure would not report a pooled percent reduction in disease index across 19 states. So the supporting reference, as given, does not contain the claimed finding.

Implications:
- The corroborating "52%" figure is either miscited or fabricated relative to the listed reference, so it provides no real external support for the trial's 42% result.
- The trial's own number (42% ±8% SE) is a reasonable, internally plausible effect for a triazole applied at anthesis, and the multi-year, multi-location design (3 years × 5 locations) is a genuine strength. But we cannot verify it from the materials, and the SE alone does not tell us about location-to-location variability or whether the reduction was statistically significant.
- Minor issue: "propiconazole + prothioconazole" — commercial Prosaro is prothioconazole + tebuconazole, so the active-ingredient description may also be inaccurate.

Confidence: LOW. The trial design is sound in principle, but the one piece of external corroboration is tied to a reference that cannot contain that result. Before raising confidence, verify the correct citation for the 52% meta-analytic figure, confirm the fungicide's actual active ingredients, and obtain the trial's significance/variance details.
