Based on the data provided, the treated watershed does show significantly greater plant species richness than the reference watershed, and the effect is large.

The statistics
- Mean richness: 28.3 vs. 19.7 species/plot, a difference of about 8.6 species/plot.
- Welch's t-test: t(21.4) = 4.73, p = 0.00013, well below the conventional 0.05 threshold.
- 95% CI for the difference [4.4, 12.8] excludes zero, consistent with the significant p-value.
- Cohen's d = 1.87, a large effect, so the difference is not just detectable but substantial in magnitude.

Welch's t-test is the appropriate choice here, since it does not assume equal variances (the SDs, 4.2 vs. 5.1, differ somewhat) and the sample sizes are equal.

What you can conclude
Within this dataset, plots in the treated watershed averaged roughly 4 to 13 more species than plots in the reference watershed, a statistically significant and large difference.

Important caveats before over-reading it
- Causation vs. correlation. This is a comparison of two watersheds, not a randomized experiment. With only one treated and one reference watershed, "treatment" is confounded with every other way the two watersheds differ (soil, hydrology, elevation, prior land use, aspect, etc.). The restoration may be responsible, but these data alone cannot isolate it; this is pseudoreplication at the watershed level, since the plots replicate within a watershed, not the treatment.
- No baseline. There is no pre-restoration measurement, so we cannot confirm the watersheds were similar before treatment or measure actual change.
- Species richness only. Richness counts species but says nothing about which species. A higher count could include non-native or weedy colonizers, so richness alone does not establish improved ecological quality.
- Single time point (3 years).

Bottom line: The data show a statistically significant, large difference in species richness favoring the treated watershed. They are consistent with a positive restoration effect but do not, on their own, prove that restoration caused it, because of the lack of watershed-level replication, no baseline, and richness being only a partial measure of biodiversity.
