The claim of consistent improvement is not well supported by the provided references.

Evidence for improvement (with caveats):
- AMF form symbioses with >80% of terrestrial plant species [1], and under P-limited conditions can raise plant P uptake by 20-80% [2]. This establishes a plausible mechanism and meaningful upper-bound effect size for low-P soils.

Evidence against "consistency":
- Verbruggen & Kiers [3] found mycorrhizal benefits are context-dependent and often suppressed under high-input agricultural conditions. This directly undercuts the idea of a reliable, uniform benefit, especially in typical fertilized cropping systems.

Two important limits of the evidence base:
1. Scope mismatch. The strongest supporting sources are not specific to annual grain crops in low-P soils. Reference [2] concerns defoliation effects on AM contribution, and [1] is a general reference text. The 20-80% range itself signals high variability, not consistency.
2. The one study cited as showing "consistent" improvement in a grain crop (Johnson 2019, winter wheat, 15 site-years) is mentioned in the excerpt but is not in the provided reference list (references stop at [3]). I therefore cannot verify or cite it, and it should not be relied upon as support.

Bottom line: The provided literature supports that mycorrhizal inoculation can substantially improve P uptake under low-P conditions, but it does not support consistency. The one explicit meta-analytic source [3] emphasizes context-dependence and suppression under high-input agriculture, and the single study claiming consistent grain-crop results is unverifiable within the cited references. The claim as stated is overstated.
