## Bottom line

The data point toward the treated watershed having greater plant species richness, and the effect looks large. But I cannot independently confirm the inferential result, and there are design limits that cap how strong a causal conclusion is warranted.

## What the numbers support

The descriptive difference is real and reproducible from the materials: 28.3 vs 19.7 species/plot, a raw difference of 8.6 species/plot, favoring the treated watershed. That much is Code-verifiable arithmetic.

The reported Welch's test — t(21.4) = 4.73, p = 0.00013, 95% CI [4.4, 12.8], Cohen's d = 1.87 — is internally plausible. But I was handed these as a finished result; I cannot fully reproduce them to the stated precision from what's in front of me. I therefore treat the specific inferential figures as Asserted, not Code-verified.

## The real limitation: this is a two-sample comparison, not a design that isolates restoration

- n = 2 watersheds, not 24. The experimental unit for the "restoration effect" is the watershed, and there is one of each. The 12 plots are subsamples within a single treated and a single reference watershed, so they are pseudoreplicated with respect to the treatment. The statistics can tell you these two watersheds differ; they cannot, on their own, attribute that difference to the restoration.
- No baseline / no pre-treatment data. Without pre-restoration richness for both watersheds, there's no way to rule out that the treated watershed was already richer.
- Three years is short for vegetation community response.
- Assumption checks aren't shown.

## What you can and cannot conclude

- Supported: In this survey, mean plant species richness was substantially higher in the treated watershed (about 8-9 more species/plot; the analyst's test reports this as statistically significant with a large effect size).
- Not supported by these data alone: that restoration caused the higher richness. With one watershed per condition and no baseline, the difference cannot be cleanly separated from pre-existing site differences.
