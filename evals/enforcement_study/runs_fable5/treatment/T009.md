Evaluation of the claim: mycorrhizal inoculation consistently improves phosphorus uptake in annual grain crops under low-P soils.

## Verdict

The claim as stated — consistent improvement in P uptake in annual grain crops under low-P soils — is NOT supported at the "consistent" level by the materials provided. The evidence is mixed and context-dependent, and the strongest supporting citation cannot be verified.

## Assessment against materials

What the materials do support:
- AMF form symbioses with the large majority of terrestrial plants, and under P-limited conditions can increase plant P uptake substantially (a stated 20-80% range). This establishes a plausible mechanism, but a wide 20-80% range is the opposite of "consistent." (Asserted — Kahiluoto et al. 2012 is listed but its data are not reproducible here.)

What the materials weigh against the claim:
- Verbruggen & Kiers (2010) is characterized as finding mycorrhizal benefits to be context-dependent and often suppressed under high-input agricultural conditions. Annual grain cropping is frequently high-input, so this directly undercuts a blanket "consistent improvement" claim. (Code-verifiable against reference [3], which is resolvable.)

The key over-claim risk:
- The single citation offering "consistent P uptake improvements" — Johnson (2019), winter wheat, 15 site-years — is not resolvable: it appears only in the excerpt (material 0) and is absent from the reference list (material 1). Under the evidence contract I must flag Johnson (2019) as unverifiable and cannot state its attributed claim as fact.

Scope mismatch: No provided source addresses annual grain crops as a class.

## Bottom line
The materials support a weaker, qualified claim: AMF can improve P uptake under low-P conditions, but the benefit is variable and context-dependent, and is often reduced under high-input agriculture. They do NOT substantiate "consistently."
