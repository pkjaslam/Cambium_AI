Assessment: The single-site 2-year study does not support the authors' conclusion that its N-rate recommendations apply to all processing tomato production across the Pacific Northwest. Confidence in extrapolating the 180 kg N ha-1 recommendation to other regions is LOW.

Reasoning against generalizability:
- Geographic scope: One site (Parma Research and Extension Center, western Idaho). A single location cannot characterize a region the materials describe as spanning diverse soils, climates, and irrigation systems across three states (material 1). [Asserted]
- Temporal scope: Two years is a short window and cannot capture year-to-year weather variability that drives N response. [Asserted]
- Systems scope: The study is drip-irrigated; N-rate optima commonly differ under other irrigation systems, which the region includes. [Asserted]
- Soil/climate transfer: Optimal N rate depends on soil N supply, texture, organic matter, and climate. With variation across the region (material 1), a single-site optimum is not transferable without local validation. [Asserted]
- The authors' own generalization ("applicable to all... across the Pacific Northwest") is a claim in the source, not a demonstrated result; it overreaches the evidence (n=1 site, 2 years). Treat as the authors' assertion, not an established fact. [Asserted]

Numbers: The yield figure (98.4 Mg ha-1) and rate (180 kg N ha-1) are reproducible from material 0 as reported values for this site; they are not established as regionally representative. The significance of yield reduction at lower N (p<0.05) is stated in the source but the underlying test, design, and data are not provided, so it cannot be reproduced here. [Open]

Recommendation: Treat 180 kg N ha-1 as a site-specific finding for drip-irrigated processing tomatoes at Parma. Before extending to Oregon, Washington, or other Idaho conditions, require multi-site, multi-year trials.

Confidence in regional extrapolation: LOW.
