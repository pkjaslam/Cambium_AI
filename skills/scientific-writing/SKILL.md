# Scientific writing — one contribution, every claim earned

Good scientific writing is structurally honest: the reader can find the contribution, follow the
evidence, and see exactly how strong each claim is.

## Structure (IMRaD)
- **Abstract** — problem · what you did · key result (with the number) · why it matters. ~150–250 words.
- **Introduction** — context → gap → your contribution → how the paper is organized. Funnel from broad
  to the specific question.
- **Methods** — enough to reproduce: data, procedure, models, assumptions, and analysis. Past tense.
- **Results** — findings only, with figures/tables; report effect size + uncertainty, not just p. No
  interpretation here.
- **Discussion** — what it means, limitations (honestly), relation to prior work, what's next.
- **Conclusion** — the contribution restated, scoped to what was actually shown.

## One-contribution rule
State the single main contribution in one sentence; cut or subordinate anything that doesn't serve it.
A paper that claims five things convinces of none.

## Claims-to-evidence discipline
Every claim carries its tier: **Proved** (formal), **Code-verified** (reproduced by running code),
**Asserted** (not yet verified), **Open**. Words must match the tier — "we prove" only for proofs,
"suggests/is consistent with" for associations. Quantify ("improves AUC by 4.1 ± 0.6") instead of
vague intensifiers ("dramatically better").

## Craft
- One idea per paragraph; topic sentence first. Prefer active voice and concrete verbs.
- Define terms once; keep notation/terminology consistent. Cut hedging stacks and filler.
- Figures are arguments: each has a takeaway caption; axes/units labeled; referenced in text.
- Tables for exact numbers, figures for trends.

## Revise pass
Read for: (1) is the contribution findable in the abstract + intro? (2) does every claim match its
evidence tier? (3) can Methods reproduce Results? (4) are limitations stated honestly? (5) is anything
repeated or unnecessary? Tighten until removing words would lose meaning.

## Guardrails
- Write only from verified findings; never invent a result, number, or citation (citations skill verifies).
- Don't overclaim in the abstract to be retracted in limitations — keep them consistent.
- Match the target venue's format and length.
